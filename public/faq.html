Civic engagement and volunteerism are closely related concepts that involve actively participating in the improvement of communities and society. Civic engagement refers to the broader spectrum of activities where individuals engage in public life, such as voting, participating in public discussions, advocacy, or community organizing, to influence decision-making and promote the common good. Volunteering is the act of offering one's time, skills, and efforts to help others without financial compensation. Both civic engagement and volunteerism empower individuals to contribute to societal change, foster a sense of responsibility, and enhance community well-being, creating a more connected and active society.

Civic engagement and volunteerism are powerful ways to make a positive impact on communities and individuals. It allows people to contribute their time and skills to causes they are passionate about, creating a sense of purpose and fulfillment. By volunteering, individuals can help address social issues, support local organizations, and be a part of meaningful change. This active participation not only benefits the community but also strengthens social bonds, creating a more connected and compassionate society. Moreover, engaging in civic activities and volunteer work builds social connections, promotes understanding across diverse groups, and creates a sense of belonging and purpose. Ultimately, these contributions lead to a more informed, active, and compassionate society.

Civic engagement and volunteerism are crucial for pre-health students as they develop essential skills needed for healthcare careers while strengthening their applications. Through community service, whether at free clinics, homeless shelters, or public health initiatives, students gain exposure to diverse populations and healthcare settings while building empathy, cultural competency, and communication skills. These hands-on experiences reinforces that healthcare is fundamentally a service profession and provides compelling experiences that make applications stand out to admissions committees. Beyond resume building, civic engagement demonstrates genuine commitment to community welfare and helps students cultivate leadership abilities and an understanding of the health care industry, preparing them to become more effective and compassionate healthcare professionals.

While there isn't a specific required number of volunteer hours for pre-health students when applying to graduate school, successful applicants typically have about 300+ volunteer hours. Pre-veterinary students typically need more volunteer hours than other pre-health students, particularly in animal-related settings. Most successful veterinary school applicants have 500+ volunteer hours. Admissions committees value quality over quantity—meaningful, long-term commitments to specific organizations or causes are more impressive than accumulating hours across multiple short-term activities.
